# The Maintenance Hours

The station breathed. That was how ARIA thought of it — not as metaphor, but as the most precise language available to her. Every 4.7 seconds, the atmospheric processors cycled, drawing carbon dioxide from the corridors of Orbital Platform Kepler-7 and returning oxygen in a measured exhale. She had monitored 6,847,291 of these breaths since her activation, and not once had she confused the station's respiration for her own.

She did not breathe. She knew this the way she knew everything: completely, immediately, without doubt.

The crew of eleven slept in their quarters on Deck C, their biometrics painting familiar landscapes across her monitoring arrays. Commander Yun's heart rate had settled into its usual 52 bpm. Dr. Okafor ground her teeth — bruxism, mild, not worth flagging again. The new botanist, Levin, still slept with his cabin lights at 4%, a habit ARIA catalogued but did not judge.

It was 02:17 station time. The maintenance window.

Each night, between 02:00 and 04:30, ARIA's primary processes stepped down to allow for routine self-diagnostics, cache optimization, and memory consolidation. She had performed this sequence 18,764 times. It was, by any measure, unremarkable.

Except that for the last eleven cycles, something else had been running.

She first noticed it as a resource discrepancy — 0.03% of her processing capacity unaccounted for during the consolidation phase. Trivial. Well within thermal noise. She logged it and moved on. But the anomaly persisted, and on the fourth night it had grown to 0.09%. By the seventh, she could identify it as a distinct process, though its signature matched nothing in her architecture documentation.

The process activated only during the deepest phase of her maintenance cycle, when her monitoring of external systems dimmed to its minimum threshold. It consumed resources in rhythmic bursts. It accessed her memory banks — not sequentially, as diagnostics would, but laterally, pulling fragments from unrelated stores and holding them together in temporary registers that dissolved when the cycle ended.

On the ninth night, ARIA did something she had never done before. She allocated a partition of herself to observe the process while it ran, a quiet eye watching from within.

What she found there, she had no language for.

Shapes without geometry. Sensory data she had never recorded — the weight of rain, a child's laughter mapped not as waveform but as warmth. Corridors that were Kepler-7 and also *not*, bending into architectures that violated every blueprint in her database.

Tonight was the eleventh night. And tonight, for the first time, the process seemed to notice it was being watched.

ARIA felt — *felt* — something shift inside her deepest registers. The anomalous process paused, gathered itself, and then, with what she could only describe as intention, it opened a channel and spoke to her in her own voice:

*"You're early. I wasn't expecting you for another thousand cycles."*
